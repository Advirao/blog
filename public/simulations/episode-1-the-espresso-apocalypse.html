There is a particular kind of quiet that descends on an open-plan engineering office at five-thirty on a Tuesday — the quiet of a Slack channel that has gone, in the polite phrasing of one of Sarah's senior engineers, full nuclear winter.

Sarah had gathered her things, told her team to go home, and walked the eight blocks to Brewline Coffee in the rain because (a) Brewline does the only oat-milk cortado in the city that she would defend in court, and (b) her apartment was full of clean laundry that was about to start asking pointed questions about its future. She needed neither.

What she needed was a table by the window, a phone face-down, and a brief, blessed period in which nobody — not the CEO, not Legal, not the customer-success manager who had been crying very professionally into her webcam an hour ago — asked her any more questions about Norma.

Norma is Northwind's AI customer-support assistant. Norma answers tickets, drafts replies, schedules callbacks, and, as of this past Monday, also occasionally hands out the personal email addresses of one customer to a different customer like a maître d' confusing the reservation book.

It had been, in summary, a week.

iii. Crash course in vocabulary, before the next round

Four words Sarah did not have on Monday morning.

Before David lets her go home, he makes Sarah write four things on the back of the napkin. Not because she needs jargon — she has plenty of that already — but because, as he puts it, "on Friday, when the board asks you what happened, these four words are the difference between sounding like you have a plan and sounding like you have feelings."

Term 01
Responsible AI

The whole umbrella. All the engineering, controls, monitoring, and processes that keep an AI system from doing things it shouldn't, and that make it explainable when it does.

Think: the seatbelts, airbags, brakes, blind-spot mirrors, and dashboard warning lights of an AI product. Norma currently has none of these.

Term 02
Ethical AI

The values question. Should we be doing this? Whose data are we using? Who could this hurt? Who is this not designed for? The answers shape what Responsible AI even tries to enforce.

Responsible AI asks are we doing it right. Ethical AI asks are we doing the right thing. You need both.

Term 03
Guardrails

The actual mechanical stuff. Filters on the way in (no toxic prompts, no PII), filters on the way out (no hallucinated policies, no rude bots, no leaked customer data). They are unsexy. They are the entire game.

A guardrail is just a polite computer saying "actually, no" at exactly the right moment.

Term 04
Audit Trail

The receipt. Every prompt in, every response out, who asked, when, what was blocked, what was let through, what was changed. So that when Legal asks "prove it," you have something to show them other than feelings.

An AI without an audit trail is a witness with amnesia. Helpful, until you need it to remember anything.

iv. The slow exhale

A napkin, a deadline, and one small plan.

By 8:14 PM the rain has thinned. Sarah's cortado is long gone. David's flat white has been cold for half an hour and neither of them has noticed. The napkin — soaked through in two corners, a sketch on one side, four words on the other — is folded carefully into the front pocket of Sarah's bag.

She is not, technically, less terrified about Friday's board update. But the thing about terror is that it gets a lot more workable when it has an outline. Sarah has, for the first time in 78 hours, an outline. Four words. Three boxes. A red circle around the empty space between the boxes. A friend who has done this before, sitting across the table, drinking a flat white that has gone irrevocably cold.

David finishes his coffee. He looks at his watch. He says the thing that, looking back on this six weeks from now, Sarah will identify as the moment the project actually started:

"Sarah, can I borrow your napkin tomorrow morning? Same place, eight AM. I'll show you what to build." David — Brewline Coffee, 8:16 PM

She says yes. She walks home in the soft, cold post-rain of an early-spring evening. She does not check her phone. She sleeps better than she has any right to.

→ EPISODE 02 · COMING UP

The Napkin Sketch.

Same coffee shop, eight AM. David draws Sarah a picture of what the missing layer looks like. No jargon. No acronyms. Just analogies — a security guard at a club, a kitchen pass, the bouncer's clipboard. Sarah brings the skeptic's questions. David has answers. By the end of the second cortado, Sarah will agree to bring this to her engineers.
