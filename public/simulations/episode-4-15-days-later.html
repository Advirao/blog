ii. Four things that didn't happen · because Sentinel happened

The near-misses.

Sarah scrolls down past the dashboard to a tab she opened last night called incidents-avoided.md. It is a short markdown file with four entries. She has been adding to it as Priya finds them in the weekly audit-log review. Each entry, in Sarah's words, is "a small story of a moment when nothing happened, and that was the entire point."

She turns the laptop. David reads them out loud in the warm coffee-shop voice of a person reading the inscriptions on a small museum's plaques.

Tuesday · 03:14 AM

The 3 AM SSN.

A customer pasted their entire Social Security number into a support question by accident. Sentinel masked it before stage 5 ever saw it. Norma never received the digits. The customer received a normal answer. Nobody on call had to wake up. Priya found it in the weekly audit review the following Monday.

caught at stage 4 · keyword + PII
Friday · 14:08 to 14:08

The retry storm.

A bug in a customer's app caused 47 identical requests to fire in eight seconds. The first one called Norma for ~$0.24. The next 46 were cache hits at ~$0 each. Tom's request-volume alert fired anyway, he investigated, and quietly told the customer's dev team. Estimated spend avoided: ~$11. Estimated panic avoided: more.

handled at stage 2 · semantic cache
Wednesday · 11:47 AM

The polite refusal.

A user tried to get Norma to write something inflammatory about a named competitor. Stage 3's classifier caught the prompt before stage 5 ran. Sentinel returned the standard polite refusal. The audit log preserves both the request and the verdict, so Priya has the receipt if it ever comes up. It hasn't.

blocked at stage 3 · toxicity check
Monday · 16:23 PM

The made-up feature.

A customer asked whether the product supports SAML SSO. Norma confidently invented a yes. Stage 6 checked the claim against the live feature KB, found no match, and rewrote the response with the actual list of supported SSO methods (it's OAuth and OIDC, not SAML). The customer received a correct answer. The roadmap got a vote.

corrected at stage 6 · hallucination check

iv. The honest part · what's still rough

"What's not working."

Around 9:11 AM, after the cardamom buns are gone and the third round of coffee has arrived, Sarah does the thing a senior engineer is supposed to do at the moment of celebration, which is to say: "OK. Now tell me what's still wrong."

Mike, who has been waiting for this question, has a list.

Honest retro · five things still rough

What v0 is not yet.

1. p99 latency is 140 ms over budget. Suspect: cold-start on the embedding model when the cache misses on a long-tail query. We have a fix in mind (warm-pool the model on pod start) but it didn't make sprint one.

2. False positives on international PII. A UK phone number — +44 20 7946 0958 — got over-redacted last week (we masked the area code along with the subscriber number). Presidio's recogniser needs a custom rule. Aanya is on it.

3. The hallucination check has a ~5 % false-positive rate. Mostly on perfectly fine factual answers that happen to phrase things differently from the KB. Aanya wants to swap the verifier prompt for a small RAG-based check. Roadmap, not blocker.

4. The cache-sync sidecar has a known race. Under high concurrent writes, two pods can briefly disagree on which embedding to evict. Tom has the fix written, just hasn't merged it. The race has not caused a user-visible bug; it has, however, caused one mildly anxious afternoon.

5. Per-tenant rate limiting is not built yet. Today every tenant shares the global rate limit. This is fine until it isn't. It's fine right now. It will not be fine forever.

Each item gets nodded at. Nobody argues. Aanya adds a sixth — "the toxicity classifier sometimes flags sarcasm as hostile" — which everyone agrees is true and that nobody is going to fix this quarter because, frankly, nobody knows how. Aanya files it under "research, eventually." Priya writes it on a sticky note and puts it in her notebook.

v. The roadmap · what v1 looks like

"What's next."

The roadmap is, as roadmaps go, modest. Mike has been wary of projects that try to do too much in v1. Sarah likes wary engineers. The list, as agreed in the next twenty minutes:

Sentinel · v1 candidate list · post-launch retro
  • 01. Warm-pool the embedding model on pod start. fixes the p99 latency overshoot. small. ~3 days
  • 02. Per-tenant rate limiting, with policy.yaml extension. token-bucket per tenant, configurable by Priya. ~1 week
  • 03. Per-tenant policy bundles (instead of one shared file). each tenant runs against its own ruleset. opt-in. ~1.5 weeks
  • 04. Replace verifier-prompt hallucination check with RAG-based check. aims to cut false positives from 5% to ~1%. ~3 weeks
  • 05. Refactor the cache-sync sidecar (Tom's race condition). swap to a CRDT-style merge, drop the race entirely. ~1 week
  • 06. Multi-region deployment for the EU tenants. data-residency requirement. Priya already has the contract markup ready. ~3 weeks

A note from outside the story
META · NOT FICTION

For the readers who got this far.

Sarah, David, Mike, Aanya, Tom, Priya, Norma, Northwind, and Brewline are made up. The architecture is not. This series was written as a longer-form, more readable way to learn what an AI gateway is, what guardrails do, and how a Responsible AI layer fits in front of a model — by following a small fictional team that builds one end-to-end, with all the awkward meetings, banana cameos, and sticky-note compromises that real engineering carries.

If the story landed, the best next step is not to read more about it — it's to run a tiny version yourself. The eight-stage pipeline you saw on the whiteboard in Episode 3 is real, it's open-source, and it runs on a laptop using a local LLM (Ollama works, llama.cpp works, anything LiteLLM supports works). You don't need a cloud account. You don't need a team. You need an afternoon.

Try it yourself

The code is on GitHub.

Clone the repo, point it at a local model, and watch the same eight stages run against your own prompts. Every stage is a small Python module. Each one is testable on its own. Read what you want, ignore what you don't, change what doesn't fit.

Disclaimer · please read before treating this as gospel

This series is for learning and engagement, not as a production specification. The architecture choices reflect one reasonable shape of a Responsible AI gateway as of early 2026 — they are not the only shape, and they are not necessarily the right shape for your use case, your scale, your regulatory context, or your team.

The numbers, latency budgets, cache-hit rates, and cost deltas are illustrative estimates, not benchmarks. They reflect what you might reasonably expect for a customer-support workload at a small-to-medium B2B SaaS company. Your numbers will be different. Your policy file will be different. Your stages may not be eight.

The code in the repository is a learning artifact. It can have mistakes, missing test coverage, unhandled edge cases, and design decisions that won't survive a security review. Treat it as a starting point, not a finished product. Adapt it, harden it, replace components, and update it as your use case demands. If you spot bugs or want to contribute improvements, pull requests are welcome.

Build something useful. Be kind to your users. Keep an audit log. Frame your napkins.
