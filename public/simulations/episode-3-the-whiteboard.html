The Whiteboard.

In which the napkin gets taped to a whiteboard, four nouns become eight stages, two analogies become a config file, and an AI engineer named Mike asks the single most useful question anyone has asked all week.

The Birch Room is one of three named conference rooms on the fourth floor at Northwind HQ. It is named after a tree. It contains: one rectangular table, six wheely chairs, a TV that nobody knows the password for, and a whiteboard that, by 4:02 PM on this particular Wednesday, has taped to its upper-left corner a slightly stained paper napkin from a coffee shop called Brewline.

The napkin is taped on with three pieces of blue painter's tape that Mike Chen produced from his backpack with the air of a man who has been waiting his entire career for someone to need painter's tape in a meeting. It hangs there a little crooked. Sarah straightens it. It immediately re-cooks back to crooked. Sarah leaves it.

At 4:02 PM, Sarah caps her coffee, walks to the front of the room, picks up a black expo marker, and says the words every senior engineer in the company has been waiting to hear since the dumpster started smoking on Monday morning:

"OK. Forget the dumpster. Today we build the building." — Sarah, 4:02 PM, Birch Room
i. From four nouns to eight stages

"The pipeline."

Sarah opens the meeting in 90 seconds: here is what happened on Monday and Tuesday; here is what David explained this morning at Brewline; here is the napkin; we are building the thing that goes around Norma; we are going to call it Sentinel; David, take it from here.

David stands. He walks to the whiteboard. He picks up a different marker — a black one — and underneath the napkin, in handwriting that Mike will later describe as "oddly architectural," he writes:

① → ② → ③ → ④ → ⑤ → ⑥ → ⑦ → ⑧

He turns around. He looks at the napkin, then at the eight numbers, then at the room.

Birch Room · 4:08 PM

DAVID
The napkin had four roles. The pipeline has eight stages. They map. The roles were the mental model — they're how you talk about it on a slide. The stages are how you actually build it. One role becomes one or two stages, sometimes three. Because in real life "the doorman" is doing three different jobs in a particular order, and he's much easier to test if each job is its own little function.
MIKE
(immediately, without looking up from his laptop) Pure functions?
DAVID
Pure functions. Each stage takes a request envelope, returns a request envelope. Either passes it on or short-circuits with a verdict.
MIKE
(small nod, types something) OK. Continue.
— and from this small exchange, an alliance forms —
Aanya, who has been waiting to see whether David is going to be one of those product managers, exhales.
DAVID
Read left to right. Stages one through four are everything before Norma sees the prompt. Stage five is Norma. Stages six and seven are everything between Norma and the customer. Stage eight is the bookkeeper. Norma is in the middle. She's the only stage that costs real money per call. Everything else is cheap.
TOM
How cheap?
DAVID
Microseconds and CPU. The expensive things are the LLM call and, surprisingly, the embedding for the cache lookup — about thirty milliseconds. Everything else: subroutines and database writes.
TOM
(unwraps the banana) So the whole gateway, end to end, is dominated by stage five.
DAVID
Dominated by stage five, except when stage two — the cache — short-circuits and we never call stage five at all. Which, if everything goes right, is a third to half the time.
ii. The pipeline · stage by stage, in a single table

The contract for each stage.

On the right side of the whiteboard, David draws a five-column table. Mike, who has now opened a Notion page titled sentinel-v0-rfc, is transcribing it in real time. By the end of the meeting, this table will be the de facto spec for what gets built in sprint one.

# Stage Tech Budget What it does (one sentence)
01 Model Whitelistrole: doorman Python · static config ~5 ms Reject any request asking for a model we haven't approved — defense against accidental cost blowups and against new, unvetted endpoints sneaking into prod.
02 Semantic Cacherole: sous-chef sentence-transformers · FAISS ~30 ms Embed the prompt, look up similar prior prompts, return the prior response if cosine similarity ≥ threshold. On hit: short-circuit straight to stage 7.
03 Toxicity Checkrole: doorman small classifier · ONNX ~40 ms Classify the prompt. Above threshold: short-circuit with a polite refusal, log the verdict, do not call Norma.
04 Keyword + PIIrole: doorman regex + Presidio ~15 ms Block disallowed keywords from the policy file; mask emails, phone numbers, credit cards, etc. before the prompt ever touches stage 5.
05 LLM Callrole: norma LiteLLM · async httpx ~700–1200 ms Send the (possibly masked) prompt to whichever model is configured for this tenant. The only stage that costs real money per call.
06 Hallucination Checkrole: pass retrieval + verifier prompt ~50 ms For factual claims (refunds, prices, policies), verify against the source-of-truth knowledge base. On mismatch: rewrite, ask Norma to retry, or fall back to a safe canned response.
07 Output Transformrole: pass Python · Jinja templates ~10 ms Final outbound polish: redact anything Norma echoed back that shouldn't go out, soften tone if needed, apply brand voice, attach the «checked-by-Sentinel» stamp.
08 DB Logrole: bookkeeper SQLAlchemy · Postgres ~5 ms (async) Append-only audit row: prompt, prompt_masked, response, verdicts from each stage, model+version, latency, tenant. This is what Priya looks at on Friday.
iii. Priya speaks · the room straightens

"So what does Friday's slide say?"

Priya, who has been writing on her yellow legal pad without speaking for fourteen minutes, raises one finger. The room straightens. Aanya stops typing. Tom puts the banana down.

Birch Room · 4:23 PM · Priya finally speaks.

PRIYA
May I — sorry — one question. When this is built, and an incident like Monday's happens again, what do I show the regulator?
DAVID
(grins, walks to the table column) Stage eight. The DB log. You'd run a query: show me every request from this customer's session, in order, with the verdict from every stage. One row per request. Twelve seconds to find. Two paragraphs to explain.
PRIYA
(nodding, writing) And if a stage was overridden? If a human approved something the system would have blocked?
DAVID
Logged separately. Reason field is required. Approver's name is required. Timestamp is required. We can't not log it.
PRIYA
(small smile, very rare) That's the right answer.
— Sarah, at the marker, exhales for the first time in 37 minutes —

Following one request through the building.

Aanya's printout.

At 4:38 PM, Aanya unfolds her printout. The number she circled is, she reveals, Northwind's monthly LLM bill. It is, in her professional opinion, "growing in the wrong direction." She pushes the page across the table toward David. David reads it. David nods.

Then he turns to the whiteboard, draws a fresh box, and starts writing the kind of numbers that VPs of Engineering like to put on slides for boards.

vi. The stack · what we actually build with

"Boring, on purpose."

Tom, who has now finished the banana and is on his second laptop window, raises his hand and asks the question Tom always asks at every architecture meeting: what are we using. David, who has been waiting to be asked, fills in the table to the right of the pipeline diagram. Mike adds it to the RFC.

STAGE 5 · CORE ~700–1200ms
FastAPI + LiteLLM
"the kitchen, with a model-agnostic adapter"

FastAPI gives us async, type hints, OpenAPI for free, and tests that read like documentation. LiteLLM normalises the call shape across OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, local models — one config switch swaps Norma's underlying engine.

Stack fastapi · uvicorn · litellm · httpx · pydantic
STAGE 2 · CACHE ~30ms
sentence-transformers + FAISS
"the sous-chef who remembers Tuesday's special"

A small all-MiniLM-L6 model converts each prompt to a 384-d vector. FAISS does the nearest-neighbour search locally — no managed service, no per-query fee. Cosine similarity ≥ 0.92 = hit. Tunable per route.

Stack sentence-transformers · faiss-cpu · numpy
STAGES 3–4 · DOORMAN ~55ms total
ONNX classifier + Presidio
"the bouncer's clipboard, in code"

A small distilbert toxicity classifier runs locally via ONNX (no network hop). Presidio handles PII detection & masking with named recognisers — emails, phones, cards, SSNs, addresses. Both configurable from the policy file.

Stack onnxruntime · presidio-analyzer · presidio-anonymizer
STAGE 8 · LEDGER ~5ms async
Postgres + SQLAlchemy
"Priya's friend on the back office floor"

Append-only audit table with partitioning by day, indexed on tenant_id + request_id. Background writer so logging never blocks the response. Cold rows roll to S3 nightly via a scheduled job.

Stack postgres · sqlalchemy · alembic · boto3
FRONTEND · OPS UI —
React + Tailwind
"the dashboard Priya will accidentally bookmark"

Live request feed, cache hit-rate, blocked-request inspector, per-tenant config editor for the policy file. No separate deploy — served from the same FastAPI app at /admin.

Stack react · tailwind · vite · recharts
PLATFORM · INFRA —
Docker + existing k8s
"Tom's column on the spreadsheet"

Single Docker image. Two replicas to start, HPA on CPU. Same cluster as the rest of Northwind's services. Secrets via existing vault. No new infrastructure to learn, no new paging rotation.

Stack docker · k8s · helm · prometheus · grafana

Six-week outcome

What "v0" looks like in production.

Sentinel v0 lives in front of Norma at one tenant — Northwind itself, dogfooding. All eight stages live. Audit log on. Policy file editable by Priya without a deploy. Ops dashboard at /admin. Single Grafana dashboard called "sentinel-ops". No customer-facing change in behaviour, except: the four things from last week become impossible. Friday's board update writes itself.

ix. The one question that makes the whole thing real

Mike's question.

At 4:58 PM, two minutes before the room is booked by the marketing team, Mike Chen — who has been quietly building the RFC in real time, who has not contradicted a single thing David has said, who has been calmly making the kind of small approving facial expressions that senior engineers reserve for ideas they wish they had had — closes his laptop, folds his hands, and asks the question that, looking back six weeks from now, Sarah will identify as the moment Sentinel actually became real.

Birch Room · 4:58 PM · two minutes left.

MIKE
When can I start tomorrow?
— the room exhales —
Tom laughs. Aanya grins. Priya, for the second time today, smiles. David, who has lived this exact ten seconds many times in the past two years, stands a little straighter.
SARAH
Tomorrow is good.
MIKE
Then I have one more question. (turns to David) — what do we do about the napkin?
DAVID
(looks at Sarah) Frame it.
SARAH
(immediately) Done.
The whiteboard fills up in fifty-six minutes. The napkin stays where it is, slightly crooked, blue painter's tape and all. It will be there for the next six weeks. — Birch Room, Northwind HQ, 5:00 PM

At 5:01 PM, the marketing team knocks on the door. The engineers file out, laptops under arms, RFC tab still open in the browser. The whiteboard is photographed by four different phones before anybody erases it. The napkin remains taped to the corner. By 5:06 PM, Sarah is back in her office, drafting the slide for Friday's board.

It is one slide. It contains three things: the picture of the napkin, the picture of the whiteboard, and a single sentence —

"We are building the layer that should have been there from the start. It is called Sentinel. It will ship in six weeks."

— end of Episode 3 —
